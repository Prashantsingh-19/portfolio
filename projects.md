## Onco-Directory

Oncologists often struggle to refer patients to the right specialist — the right person exists, but there's no trusted, noise-free channel to find and reach them. I designed Onco-Directory to close that gap: a verified professional network built exclusively for oncology.

The core product decisions were around trust and discovery. Verification is tied to medical license IDs — no manual approval gate, no spam. Once inside, doctors get a feed to share and discuss with peers, a graph-based connection engine that maps professionals by shared specialisation and suggests who to connect with next, and a direct messaging layer for referrals and consultations. Everything is built around the assumption that a doctor's time is scarce and their trust is hard to earn.

Currently a working prototype. Verification and trust features are the next build milestone.

## OncoAssist

Pharma strategy teams tracking competitor pipelines are largely doing it manually — pulling trial data, mapping it by drug and cancer type, trying to read intent from scattered evidence. By the time the picture is clear, the decision window has moved.

I designed OncoAssist to make that competitive read faster and more structured. The platform maps a drug's complete trial trajectory — completed trials, active trials, new initiations — so a strategy team can see at a glance where a competitor is in their journey. The insight that shaped the core design was this: once a company hits its efficacy targets, new trial volume drops and approval investment goes up. That shift is visible in the data if you know where to look.

Beyond volume, the platform surfaces what cancer types they're targeting and how their trials are structured — which tells you whether they're gunning to replace the current standard of care or positioning for combination therapy. Two very different competitive threats, two very different strategic responses.

Currently built as an analytical prototype using ClinicalTrials.gov data.
